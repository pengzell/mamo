### Motivation      
                                                                                                              
Intergenerational mobility -- the degree to which people can succeed regardless of family background -- is a cornerstone of equal opportunity. Traditionally, education has been seen as the main lever for upward mobility, but university enrollment has plateaued in many developed countries since the early 2000s, and college wage premiums have stagnated or declined. Despite the centrality of changing employment structures in explanations of wage inequality, the labor market itself has been largely absent from mobility research. MaMo aims to correct this by centering employers and workplaces as key sites where economic advantage is inherited across generations.

### Comparative Design and Data

The project uses at its core high-quality administrative data in Sweden and other countries. In the latest iteration, the team is building a large-scale collaboration involving researchers with administrative data in up to 20 different countries. These are supplemented by cross-national surveys, longitudinal panel data, labor force surveys, digital trace data, and business statistics. 
                  
### Subproject 1: Sorting to Employers                                                                          
   
This subproject asks: how and why do children from privileged backgrounds end up at higher-paying firms? It is divided into four tasks:

  - 1.1 Local labor markets -- Decomposing national-level intergenerational persistence into contributions    
  from different regions and industries. Regional polarization and manufacturing decline may propagate into
  greater income persistence. Uses shift-share instruments and decomposition techniques following Torche and  
  Corvalan (2018).
  - 1.2 Referral networks -- Studying how parents' social capital shapes children's job access. Uses
  administrative data to trace parents' former classmates and coworkers, testing whether children are more    
  likely to be hired at firms where a parental contact works versus a "placebo" contact (same workplace/degree
   but no temporal overlap). Also examines direct family co-working and business inheritance using            
  instrumental variables from sibling sex composition.
  - 1.3 Institutional pipelines -- Examining how elite educational institutions channel graduates toward
  specific high-status employers through old-boy networks, on-campus recruiting, and prestige screening. Uses 
  LinkedIn data across many countries.
  - 1.4 Class bias in hiring -- Testing whether managers favor candidates from similar class backgrounds using
   event-study designs around manager turnover in administrative data.                                        
   
### Subproject 2: Career Progression                                                                            
                  
This subproject shifts from initial job placement to what happens over a career:                            
   
  - 2.1 Mapping career trajectories -- Fitting growth curve models to earnings trajectories, decomposing      
  contributions of individual attributes, occupation, industry, sector, and internal/external job shifts. Uses
   the Comparative Panel File (Australia, Germany, Russia, South Korea, Sweden, Switzerland, UK, US) for      
  cross-country breadth.
  - 2.2 Internal labor markets -- Studying whether opportunities for within-firm promotion have declined over
  time, driven by trends like subcontracting, offshoring, and leaner workforces. Exploits Austrian            
  administrative data going back to the 1970s to trace both actual career ladders and the organizational
  structure of positions.                                                                                     
  - 2.3 Access to mentors -- Testing whether workers who share a class background with their manager, or who
  share social settings (same neighborhood, children at same school), receive faster wage growth -- an        
  empirical test of Weberian "patrimonialism."
  - 2.4 Weathering career shocks -- Examining whether workers from disadvantaged backgrounds are more         
  vulnerable to recessions, layoffs, illness, and parental leave, using event-study designs. Greater financial
   stability, less debt, and broader networks among privileged workers may buffer these shocks.
                                                                                                              
### Subproject 3: Mechanisms and Policy                                                                         
   
This subproject evaluates what can be done:                                                                 
                  
  - 3.1 Job search strategies -- Documenting how methods of job-finding (formal applications, networks,       
  referrals) differ by social background across countries and time, using labor force surveys and the ISSP.
  - 3.2 Labor market policy -- Coupling mobility statistics with longitudinal policy data (minimum wages,     
  employment protection, collective bargaining, right-to-work laws) at the state/country level. Uses          
  difference-in-differences with staggered treatment timing methods (Roth et al., 2023). European analysis
  draws on OECD/Eurostat/ICTWSS data; US analysis uses Opportunity Insights and the Correlates of State Policy
   database.      
  - 3.3 Managerial practices -- Estimating the effect of employer practices (pay transparency, worker
  representation, promotion systems, performance pay, diversity initiatives) on pay equity. Uses MOPS (US,    
  linked to admin data), WERS (UK), and ESWS (9 European countries), with marginal structural models and the
  "gap-closing estimand" (Lundberg, 2022) to handle confounding.                                              
  - 3.4 Blind hiring -- Evaluating the UK civil service's 2015 adoption of name-blind recruitment using a
  difference-in-differences design with internal personnel records and LinkedIn data.                         
   
### Methods                                                                                                     

The project deploys a wide toolkit: AKM wage decomposition, quasi-experimental designs, event studies around manager turnover and career shocks, within-family (sibling) comparisons, instrumental variables from sibling sex composition, network analysis with placebo contacts, growth curve and multilevel models, shift-share instruments, decomposition methods, difference-in-differences with staggered timing, and counterfactual simulations.
